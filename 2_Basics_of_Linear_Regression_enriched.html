Lecture 2: Basics of Linear Regression¶

Daisuke Fukuda (Textbook: Kmenta, Elements of Econometrics, 2nd ed., Chapters 7 and 10)

Learning Objectives & Roadmap¶

By the end of this lecture you should be able to:

  1. Write the linear regression model in scalar and matrix form and state the classical assumptions.
  2. Derive the ordinary least squares (OLS) estimator and interpret it as an orthogonal projection.
  3. Explain the finite-sample properties (unbiasedness, the Gauss–Markov theorem, efficiency) and the large-sample properties (consistency, asymptotic normality).
  4. Relate OLS to maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) under normality.
  5. Construct confidence intervals, carry out hypothesis tests, and form predictions together with their uncertainty.

Why this matters for project / policy evaluation. Regression is the workhorse for measuring how an outcome (land price, travel demand, household income, …) responds to explanatory factors. But a regression coefficient measures association, not causation: it answers "how does $Y$ co-move with $x$?", not "what happens to $Y$ if we intervene on $x$?". Lectures 3–8 build on this foundation to recover causal effects of infrastructure projects and policies.

Notation convention. Capital $Y$ = random variable; lowercase $y$ = its realized (observed) value; bold lowercase $\mathbf{y}$ = vector; bold capital $\mathbf{X}$ = matrix; prime $'$ = transpose; hat $\hat{\cdot}$ = estimator.

1. What Is Linear Regression?¶

$Y$ : effect (response / dependent variable) — a scalar (e.g., ice-cream consumption, land price, traffic volume).

$\mathbf{x}$ : causes (explanatory / independent variables) — a vector, for example

  • $x_{1}$ : temperature
  • $x_{2}$ : economic status
  • $x_{3}$ : age, etc.

Linear regression describes how the conditional mean of $Y$ moves with the explanatory variables: $$E[Y \mid \mathbf{x}] = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \beta_q x_q .$$ The coefficient $\beta_j = \partial E[Y\mid\mathbf{x}]/\partial x_j$ is the marginal effect of $x_j$, holding the other regressors fixed ("ceteris paribus"). The gap between the realized $Y$ and its conditional mean is the error term $\varepsilon$, which absorbs every other influence on $Y$ not captured by the regressors.

(Caution) Correlation vs. causation.

  • A fitted regression uncovers correlation / conditional association only.
  • A causal reading of $\beta_j$ further requires that $x_j$ be uncorrelated with $\varepsilon$ (exogeneity). In observational data this usually fails because of omitted variables, simultaneity, or measurement error.
  • Recovering causal effects is precisely the agenda of the following lectures (causal effect analysis, instrumental variables, panel data, matching, quasi-experiments).

2. The Regression Model¶

Data-generating process for observation $i$: $$Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_{i1} + \beta_2 X_{i2} + \cdots + \beta_q X_{iq} + \varepsilon_i, \qquad i = 1,2,\ldots,n.$$

  • $Y_i$ : dependent variable for observation $i$
  • $X_{ij}$ : $j$-th independent variable for $i$
  • $\varepsilon_i$ : error (disturbance) term
  • $\beta_0,\ldots,\beta_q$ : unknown coefficient parameters

Key points.

  • The model is linear in the parameters $\beta_j$, not necessarily in the variables. Transformations such as $x^2$, $\ln x$, or interactions $x_1 x_2$ are allowed, because they enter linearly through their coefficients.
  • $\beta_0$ is the intercept — the conditional mean of $Y$ when all regressors equal zero.
  • Number of observations $= n$.
  • Number of parameters $= q + 1 \equiv p$.

3. Matrix & Vector Notation¶

Stacking all $n$ observations:

$$\mathbf{y}=\begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ \vdots \\ y_n \end{bmatrix},\quad \mathbf{X}=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & X_{11} & X_{12} & \cdots & X_{1q} \\ \vdots & & & \cdots & \vdots \\ 1 & X_{n1} & X_{n2} & \cdots & X_{nq} \end{bmatrix},\quad \boldsymbol{\beta}=\begin{bmatrix} \beta_0 \\ \vdots \\ \beta_q \end{bmatrix},\quad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}=\begin{bmatrix} \varepsilon_1 \\ \vdots \\ \varepsilon_n \end{bmatrix}$$

Model: $$\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$$

Dimensions. $\mathbf{y}$ is $n\times 1$, $\mathbf{X}$ is $n\times p$, $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is $p\times 1$, $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ is $n\times 1$ (with $p=q+1$).

Identification (full-rank condition). We require $\operatorname{rank}(\mathbf{X})=p$ — the columns of $\mathbf{X}$ are linearly independent (which needs $n\ge p$). This makes $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ invertible and the coefficients uniquely determined. Perfect multicollinearity among regressors violates it.

Note: capital $Y_i$ is the dependent variable as a random variable; lowercase bold $\mathbf{y}=(y_1,\dots,y_n)'$ is its vector of realized observations (capital $\mathbf{X}$ denotes the design matrix).

4. Classical (Gauss–Markov) Assumptions¶

(A0) Full rank. $\operatorname{rank}(\mathbf{X})=p$ — no perfect multicollinearity.

(A1) Zero-mean error / exogeneity. $E(\varepsilon_i)=0\ \forall i$, i.e. $E(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})=\mathbf{0}$. The stronger strict exogeneity $E(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\mid\mathbf{X})=\mathbf{0}$ is what makes OLS unbiased and lets $\beta_j$ be read causally.

(A2) Homoskedasticity. $E(\varepsilon_i^2)=\sigma^2\ \forall i$ — Constant error variance across observations.

(A3) No autocorrelation. $Cov(\varepsilon_i,\varepsilon_j)=E(\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j)=0\ \forall i\neq j$.

Assumptions 2 and 3 combine into the spherical-error condition: $$V(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}) = E(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}') = \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}.$$

(A4) Normality (optional). $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim N(\mathbf{0},\ \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})$. Required for exact finite-sample $t$- and $F$-distributions and for MLE; not needed for OLS to be the best linear unbiased estimator.

What happens if an assumption fails (preview).

Violated Consequence for OLS Typical solutions (optional)
A2 Homoskedasticity still unbiased; usual SEs invalid robust (White) SEs, WLS/GLS
A3 No autocorrelation still unbiased; usual SEs invalid HAC SEs, GLS
A1 Exogeneity ($Cov(x,\varepsilon)\neq0$) biased and inconsistent IV, panel, matching, … (Lectures 3–8)

5. Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)¶

OLS chooses $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ to minimize the sum of squared residuals: $$\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} S(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \sum_{i=1}^n \left(y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 X_{i1} - \beta_2 X_{i2} - \cdots - \beta_q X_{iq}\right)^2 = (\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})'(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}).$$

Expanding the quadratic form, $$S(\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \mathbf{y}'\mathbf{y} - 2\boldsymbol{\beta}'\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y} + \boldsymbol{\beta}'\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}.$$

Because $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ is positive semidefinite and $S(\cdot)$ is a convex function, so the first-order condition characterizes the global minimum: $$\frac{\partial S(\boldsymbol{\beta})}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}} = \mathbf{0}.$$

OLS Estimator¶

Differentiating and setting the gradient to zero gives the normal equations: $$\frac{\partial S(\boldsymbol{\beta})}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}} = -2\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y} + 2\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} = \mathbf{0} \;\Longrightarrow\; \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y}.$$

Under (A0), $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}$ is invertible, hence $$\boxed{\;\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y}\;}$$

Geometric interpretation.

  • The fitted values are $\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{X}\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{y}$ with the projection ("hat") matrix $\mathbf{P}=\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'$.

  • The residual vector is $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{y}-\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{M}\mathbf{y}$ with $\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{P}$.

  • OLS orthogonally projects $\mathbf{y}$ onto the column space of $\mathbf{X}$, so $\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{e}=\mathbf{0}$: residuals are orthogonal to every regressor.

  • Both $\mathbf{P}$ and $\mathbf{M}$ are symmetric and idempotent.

6. Variance–Covariance Matrix of $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$¶

Substitute the model $\mathbf{y}=\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}+\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ into the estimator: $$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y} = \boldsymbol{\beta} + (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}.$$

Then, conditional on $\mathbf{X}$ and using $V(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})=\sigma^2\mathbf{I}$, $$V(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) = E\!\left[(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}-\boldsymbol{\beta})(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}-\boldsymbol{\beta})'\right] = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\,(\sigma^2\mathbf{I})\,\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} = \sigma^2 (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}.$$

The square roots of the diagonal entries are the standard errors of the coefficients (after replacing the unknown $\sigma^2$ by its estimate $s^2$, introduced later). They quantify the sampling precision of each $\hat{\beta}_j$.

7. Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)¶

Now we add the normality assumption (A4): $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim N(\mathbf{0},\ \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})$.

The joint probability density of the errors is $$f(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi\sigma^2)^{n/2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}'\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}}{2\sigma^2}\right).$$

which is regarded as a function of the unknown parameters $(\boldsymbol{\beta},\sigma^2)$ given the data, this is the likelihood.

Because products are awkward, we maximize its logarithm, the log-likelihood (substituting $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}=\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}$): $$\begin{aligned} L(\boldsymbol{\beta}, \sigma^2) &= -\frac{n}{2}\ln 2\pi - \frac{n}{2}\ln\sigma^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}'\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\\ &= -\frac{n}{2}\ln 2\pi - \frac{n}{2}\ln\sigma^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})'(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}). \end{aligned}$$

Intuition. Maximizing $L$ over $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ means minimizing $(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})'(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})$ — exactly the OLS objective. So under normality the ML and OLS estimators of $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ coincide.

Maximum Likelihood Estimator¶

The first-order conditions $$\frac{\partial L}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}} = \mathbf{0}, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial \sigma^2} = 0$$ yield $$\Rightarrow\quad \tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y}, \qquad \tilde{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n}(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}})'(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}}).$$

The slope/intercept estimator $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ is identical to the OLS $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$; the variance estimator $\tilde{\sigma}^2$ divides by $n$ (not $n-p$), which we revisit below.

OLS and ML Estimators¶

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y}, \qquad V(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) = V(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) = \sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}.$$

Note.

  • The OLS and ML estimators of $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ are equivalent (under normality).
  • OLS estimates only $\boldsymbol{\beta}$; the error variance is estimated separately.
  • ML estimates both $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ and $\sigma^2$ jointly.

8. Finite (Small) Sample Properties of OLS¶

1. Unbiasedness¶

An estimator is unbiased if $E(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) = \boldsymbol{\theta}$.

From $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \boldsymbol{\beta} + (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ and exogeneity $E(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\mid\mathbf{X})=\mathbf{0}$, we have: $$E(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\mid\mathbf{X}) = \boldsymbol{\beta} + (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\,E(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\mid\mathbf{X}) = \boldsymbol{\beta}.$$

2. Best Linear Unbiasedness (Gauss–Markov theorem)¶

Among all linear unbiased estimators $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{y}$, OLS has the smallest variance — it is BLUE (Best Linear Unbiased Estimator).

Proof sketch. Write any competing linear unbiased estimator as $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = [(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}' + \mathbf{C}]\mathbf{y}$. Unbiasedness for all $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ forces $\mathbf{C}\mathbf{X}=\mathbf{0}$. A direct computation then gives $$V(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) = \sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} + \sigma^2\mathbf{C}\mathbf{C}' = V(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) + \sigma^2\mathbf{C}\mathbf{C}',$$ and since $\mathbf{C}\mathbf{C}'$ is positive semidefinite, $V(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) - V(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) \succeq \mathbf{0}$. $\;\square$

(Note on MSE.) For a vector estimator the criterion is the mean-squared-error (MSE) matrix $E[(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}-\boldsymbol{\theta})(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}-\boldsymbol{\theta})']$ (or its trace); for a scalar it reduces to $E[(\hat{\theta}-\theta)^2] = \text{Var} + \text{Bias}^2$.

3. Efficiency¶

An estimator is efficient if it attains the minimum variance among all (not only linear) unbiased estimators. If $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ is normal (A4), OLS reaches the Cramér–Rao lower bound, so it is efficient.

9. Large-Sample (Asymptotic) Properties of OLS¶

These describe behaviour as the sample size $n \to \infty$.

1. Asymptotic unbiasedness¶

The bias vanishes in the limit: $$\lim_{n\to\infty} E(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_n) = \boldsymbol{\theta}.$$ OLS is (exactly) unbiased, hence trivially asymptotically unbiased.

2. Consistency¶

An estimator is consistent if it concentrates on the truth as $n$ grows. A sufficient condition is asymptotic unbiasedness together with vanishing variance: $$\lim_{n\to\infty} E(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_n) = \boldsymbol{\theta} \quad\text{and}\quad \lim_{n\to\infty} Var(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_n) = \mathbf{0} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \operatorname*{plim}_{n\to\infty} \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_n = \boldsymbol{\theta}.$$ For OLS, $V(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}})=\sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} = \tfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\left(\tfrac{\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}}{n}\right)^{-1} \to \mathbf{0}$ provided $\tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}\to\mathbf{Q}$ (a finite, positive-definite matrix). Hence OLS is consistent even without normality.

3. Asymptotic normality¶

By a central limit theorem, regardless of the error distribution, $$\sqrt{n}\,(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} - \boldsymbol{\beta}) \;\xrightarrow{d}\; N\!\left(\mathbf{0},\ \sigma^2 \mathbf{Q}^{-1}\right), \qquad \mathbf{Q} = \operatorname*{plim} \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X}.$$ This justifies using normal/$t$ critical values in large samples even when A4 does not hold.

4. Asymptotic efficiency¶

OLS attains the asymptotic Cramér–Rao bound, so it is asymptotically efficient.

10. Unbiased Estimator of $\sigma^{2}$¶

1. Derivation¶

The OLS residual vector is $$\mathbf{e} \equiv \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \mathbf{M}\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{M}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, \qquad \mathbf{M} = \mathbf{I} - \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'.$$ (The last equality uses $\mathbf{M}\mathbf{X}=\mathbf{0}$.) Since $\mathbf{M}$ is symmetric and idempotent, $$E(\mathbf{e}'\mathbf{e}) = E(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}'\mathbf{M}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}) = \sigma^2\,\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{M}) = \sigma^2\,(n-p),$$ because $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{M}) = n - \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}) = n - p$. Therefore $$E\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{e}'\mathbf{e}}{n-p}\right) = \sigma^2 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \boxed{\,s^2 = \frac{\mathbf{e}'\mathbf{e}}{n-p}\,}$$ is unbiased. The divisor $n-p$ is the degrees of freedom: $p$ are "used up" in estimating $\boldsymbol{\beta}$.

2. Property of the ML Estimator $\tilde{\sigma}^{2}$¶

The ML variance estimator divides by $n$: $$\tilde{\sigma}^2 = \frac{(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}})'(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}})}{n} = \frac{\mathbf{e}'\mathbf{e}}{n} = \frac{n-p}{n}\,s^2.$$ Hence $E(\tilde{\sigma}^2) = \tfrac{n-p}{n}\sigma^2 < \sigma^2$: it is negatively biased (it underestimates $\sigma^2$).

The bias factor $\tfrac{n-p}{n}\to 1$, so $\tilde{\sigma}^2$ is consistent and the bias is negligible in large samples.

11. Confidence Interval for $\boldsymbol{\beta}$¶

A confidence interval gives a formal measure of the precision/reliability of an estimated coefficient.

From $V(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) = \sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$, take the diagonal elements $$\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1} = (q_{11}, q_{22}, \ldots, q_{pp}),$$ so that, under normality, $\hat{\beta}_j \sim N(\beta_j,\ \sigma^2 q_{jj})$.

Because $\sigma$ is unknown we replace it with $s$; standardizing then turns the normal into a Student's $t$ (the extra uncertainty from estimating $\sigma$ widens the tails): $$\hat{\beta}_j \sim N(\beta_j, \sigma^2 q_{jj}) \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{\sigma\sqrt{q_{jj}}} \sim N(0,1) \;\longrightarrow\; \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{s\sqrt{q_{jj}}} \sim t_{n-p}.$$

With probability $(1-\lambda)$, $$-t_{n-p,\,\lambda/2} \le \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{s\sqrt{q_{jj}}} \le +t_{n-p,\,\lambda/2},$$ which rearranges into the confidence interval $$\hat{\beta}_j - t_{n-p,\,\lambda/2}\, s\sqrt{q_{jj}} \;\le\; \beta_j \;\le\; \hat{\beta}_j + t_{n-p,\,\lambda/2}\, s\sqrt{q_{jj}}.$$

  • $1-\lambda$: confidence level — the long-run frequency with which such intervals cover the true $\beta_j$.
  • $\lambda$: significance level — commonly 5% or 1% (giving 95% or 99% confidence).

(Interpretation caveat.) The randomness is in the interval, not in $\beta_j$: a 95% interval means 95% of intervals constructed this way contain the fixed true value.

12. Statistical Hypothesis Testing¶

  • The (two-sided) null hypothesis of "no effect" is $H_0:\ \beta_j = 0$, with alternative $H_1:\ \beta_j \neq 0$.
  • We know $\dfrac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{s\sqrt{q_{jj}}} \sim t_{n-p}$.
  • Under $H_0$ this becomes the $t$-statistic $$t_j = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j}{s\sqrt{q_{jj}}} \sim t_{n-p},$$ which follows a $t$-distribution with $(n-p)$ degrees of freedom. $$t_j = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j}{s\sqrt{q_{jj}}} \sim t_{n-p} \;\longrightarrow\; -t_{n-p,\,\lambda/2} \le t_j \le +t_{n-p,\,\lambda/2}.$$

If the $t$-value is so far from zero that it would occur by chance only, say, 5% of the time, we reject $H_0$.

Typical critical values for $\lambda = 0.05$ (5% significance / 95% confidence): $$t_{n-p,\,\lambda/2} = 1.96 \ (\text{if } n-p=\infty), \qquad = 2.00 \ (\text{if } n-p=60).$$

Practical notes.

  • $p$-value: the probability, under $H_0$, of a $t$-statistic at least as extreme as observed; reject when $p < \lambda$. Equivalently, reject when $0$ lies outside the confidence interval — testing and interval estimation are two views of the same calculation.
  • Joint tests: to test several coefficients simultaneously (e.g., $H_0:\beta_1=\beta_2=0$) use an $F$-test.
  • Goodness of fit: the coefficient of determination $R^2 = 1 - \dfrac{\mathbf{e}'\mathbf{e}}{\sum_i (y_i-\bar y)^2}$ reports the share of variance explained, but a high $R^2$ does not imply a correct or causal model.

13. Prediction with Linear Regression¶

1. Settings¶

Model and assumptions: $$\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, \qquad E(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})=\mathbf{0},\quad E(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}')=\sigma^2\mathbf{I}.$$

Estimate by OLS/ML: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y}$.

For a future/new unit with regressor vector $\mathbf{x}_f$, the point prediction is $$\hat{Y}_f = \mathbf{x}_f'\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}.$$

It is useful to distinguish two questions: predicting the mean $E[Y_f\mid\mathbf{x}_f]=\mathbf{x}_f'\boldsymbol{\beta}$ (a confidence interval), versus predicting an individual outcome $Y_f$ (a wider prediction interval that also includes the new error $\varepsilon_f$).

2. Strict Model of Future Prediction¶

$$Y_f = \mathbf{x}_f'\boldsymbol{\beta} + \varepsilon_f$$

  • $\mathbf{x}_f$ : independent variable(s) assumed in the future
  • $Y_f$ : dependent variable in the future (a random variable)
  • $\varepsilon_f$ : future error

The task is to obtain $\hat{Y}_f$, an estimate/predictor of $Y_f$, and to quantify its error.

14. Best Linear Unbiased Prediction (BLUP)¶

A desirable predictor satisfies:

  1. Linearity: $\hat{Y}_f = \mathbf{a}'\mathbf{y}$.
  2. Unbiasedness: $E(\hat{Y}_f - Y_f) = 0$.
  3. Best: it minimizes the mean squared prediction error (equivalently, the variance of the prediction error): $$\min.\ E\!\left[(\hat{Y}_f - Y_f)^2\right].$$

1. Assumption¶

$$Y_f = \mathbf{x}_f'\boldsymbol{\beta} + \varepsilon_f,$$ in analogy with $E(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})=\mathbf{0},\ E(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}')=\sigma^2\mathbf{I}$, so that $$E(\varepsilon_f) = 0, \qquad E(\varepsilon_f^2) = \sigma^2, \qquad E(\varepsilon_f \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}) = \mathbf{0}.$$

2. Result¶

The BLUP coincides with the plug-in prediction $$\hat{Y}_f = \mathbf{x}_f'\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}},$$ and the variance of the prediction error is $$\hat{\sigma}_f^2 = \sigma^2\!\left[\,\underbrace{1}_{\text{new shock }\varepsilon_f} + \underbrace{\mathbf{x}_f'(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{x}_f}_{\text{estimation uncertainty in }\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}}\right].$$

Reading the two terms.

  • Even with perfectly known $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ (second term $=0$) an irreducible error of variance $\sigma^2$ remains because of the future shock $\varepsilon_f$.
  • The second term adds the uncertainty from having estimated $\boldsymbol{\beta}$, and it grows when $\mathbf{x}_f$ lies far from the centre of the observed data — extrapolation is less reliable.

16. Summary & Connection to the Next Lecture¶

Key takeaways.

  • The OLS estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}=(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{y}$ is the orthogonal projection of $\mathbf{y}$ onto the regressors.
  • Under (A0)–(A3) OLS is unbiased and BLUE (Gauss–Markov); adding normality (A4) makes it efficient and gives exact $t$/$F$ inference.
  • Asymptotically, OLS is consistent and asymptotically normal under weak conditions, so inference is approximately valid in large samples even without normality.
  • $s^2=\mathbf{e}'\mathbf{e}/(n-p)$ is the unbiased variance estimator; confidence intervals and $t$-tests follow from $V(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}})=\sigma^2(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{X})^{-1}$.
  • Prediction error combines irreducible noise ($\sigma^2$) and estimation uncertainty.

The crucial limitation → next lecture. Every result above rests on exogeneity (A1): $\mathbf{X}$ uncorrelated with $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$. When an omitted variable, reverse causality, or measurement error breaks this, OLS is biased and a coefficient is not a causal effect. Lecture 3 (Basics of Causal Effect Analysis) formalizes what a causal effect is and how it differs from a regression slope.

References. Kmenta, Elements of Econometrics (2nd ed.), Ch. 7 (single-equation regression) and Ch. 10 (generalized/large-sample results).
